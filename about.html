The story

Why I built this.

I started noticing a pattern. Educators were being asked to teach students how to use AI responsibly — without any clear framework for what that actually meant. Parents were worried but did not know what to say. Students were either using AI for everything or avoiding it entirely, with not much in between.

What was missing was not another policy or another warning. It was a structured way to think. That is where the READY and FOCUS frameworks came from. Not from a research paper — from watching real students in real classrooms struggle with questions that had no good anchor point.

The journals came next. I wanted something a child could hold in their hands. Something that did not require a device, a login, or a subscription. A printed journal with a coloring page and a reflection prompt is not glamorous, but it works — because it forces the student to slow down and make a decision with their own brain before they move on.

The Steal This Prompt section grew out of a different observation: most people do not know why their prompts fail or succeed. They try something, it works, they move on. A prompt journal changes that. If you write down what you tried, what you changed, and what the result was — you start building real skill instead of relying on luck.

Everything on this site exists because a student, a teacher, or a parent needed it and nothing quite like it existed yet. That is still the filter I use: does this help someone think more clearly? If yes, it gets built.

How I think about AI

Three things I believe
about AI and learning.

AI is a mirror, not a mind
AI reflects the patterns in its training data. It does not reason, feel, or understand. Teaching students this early prevents both blind trust and irrational fear — and it is the foundation of everything else.
The skill that transfers everywhere
Learning to ask a clear, specific question is one of the most transferable skills a student can develop. It makes them better at school, better at problem-solving, and eventually better at work. AI just makes the feedback loop faster.
The adult in the room matters
A student journal without a teacher or parent asking follow-up questions is just paper. The conversation around the activity is where the learning actually happens. That is why every resource includes discussion prompts and why the free tools are designed to spark dialogue.
