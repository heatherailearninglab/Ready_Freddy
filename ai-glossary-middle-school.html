AI Literacy Glossary for Middle School

30 Key Terms in Plain Language | Grades 6-8

AI Kairos Learning Lab | aikairos.org

This glossary defines 30 key AI terms in clear, simple language for middle school students. Print it, post it, or keep it in your binder for reference. Every definition is written to be understood without a technical background.

Core AI Terms

Artificial Intelligence (AI)
Computer programs that can do tasks that usually require human thinking, like sorting information, answering questions, or creating text.
Algorithm
A set of step-by-step instructions that tells a computer how to complete a task or solve a problem.
Automation
When a computer or machine does a task without a person doing it manually each time.
Chatbot
A program that responds to text or voice input in a conversational way. Some chatbots use AI to generate their responses.
Data
Information that computers use to learn, make decisions, or produce outputs. Data can be text, numbers, images, audio, or video.
Generative AI
AI that creates new content like text, images, code, or music based on patterns it learned from training data.
Large Language Model (LLM)
A type of AI trained on large amounts of text. It predicts the most likely next word in a sentence to generate responses.
Machine Learning
A way computers learn from data by finding patterns, without being given exact instructions for every situation.
Natural Language Processing (NLP)
The part of AI that helps computers understand, interpret, and generate human language.
Training Data
The information used to teach an AI system. The quality and variety of training data affects what the AI learns and how it responds.

Using AI

Prompt
The words you give an AI tool to tell it what to do. A clear prompt leads to a more useful response.
Output
What the AI produces after receiving a prompt. Outputs can be text, images, code, summaries, or other content.
Input
What you give to an AI tool. Your prompt, uploaded files, or other information you provide.
Iteration
The process of revising and improving your prompt or the AI output. Good results often take more than one try.
Context
Background information that helps AI understand what you need. Adding context to your prompt makes the output more useful.
FOCUS Framework
A structured way to write prompts: Function, Objective, Context, User, Specifics. Helps you think clearly before you prompt.
READY Framework
A decision tool for responsible AI use: Recognize, Examine, Allow, Develop, Your thinking. Helps you decide if AI is appropriate for a task.

Accuracy and Limitations

Hallucination
When AI generates information that sounds real and specific but is completely made up. This includes fake facts, fake quotes, and fake sources.
Bias
When AI outputs unfairly favor or disadvantage certain groups, perspectives, or ideas. Bias comes from uneven training data and design choices.
Accuracy
Whether the information AI produces is correct. AI does not check its own accuracy. That responsibility belongs to you.
Verification
The process of checking whether AI output is correct by comparing it to reliable sources.
Misinformation
False or inaccurate information, whether created intentionally or accidentally. AI can produce misinformation without meaning to.
Deepfake
AI-generated media (video, audio, or images) designed to look or sound like a real person doing or saying something they did not.

Ethics and Responsibility

Disclosure
Being open and honest about when and how you used AI in your work.
Academic Integrity
Being honest about your work in school. This includes disclosing AI use and making sure your submitted work reflects your own understanding.
Responsible Use
Using AI thoughtfully, honestly, and with purpose. Checking outputs, disclosing use, and staying in charge of your own learning.
Transparency
Being clear and open about how AI was used, what data it was trained on, and how decisions were made.
Consent
Permission. In AI, this often refers to whether people agreed to have their data used to train AI systems.
Privacy
The right to control your personal information. AI tools may collect, store, or use data you provide.
Ethical AI
AI that is designed and used in ways that are fair, transparent, and respectful of people's rights and well-being.
